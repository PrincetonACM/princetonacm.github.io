About ACM

Welcome new workshop chairs! We're really excited to have you with us at ACM, and this will be a brief document to outline the organization of the club and our workshop program.

First, note that we're one of the groups changing the COS experience here at Princeton. Our events help bring together students, promote research in certain areas, and bring awareness to everything from projects to career paths! As an ACM officer, you'll be able to meet more people on campus and help shape the CS experience at Princeton. ACM also forms a robust network between schools and is recognized outside of Princeton (in fact, it's the largest and most well-known CS society). We're also excited to work with other groups on campus to make events happen (such as COS Council, PCA, the Career Center, Hoagie, etc.)!

Event Types

Here are some event ideas we've hosted in the past:

  • Study Breaks: We'll host ACM study breaks with trivia questions, snacks and/or boba, and prizes!
  • Research and Graduate School: August will host a talk discussing whether graduate school is right for students and the benefits of undergraduate research even if students do not plan to attend graduate school. This event was a large success last year, and we'd likely run it similarly.
  • Graduate Student Panel: A set of graduate students in ECE and COS (likely including August and Freddy) will talk about their experience, answer questions about why they chose graduate school, and answer audience questions!
  • Dinner with Graduate Students: We'll invite undergraduates for a paid meal with ACM's graduate students to ask questions and discuss graduate school.
  • Research Talks: Faculty, graduate, and undergraduate students can share their research with a large audience or introduce an audience to their field (ex. David Yan volunteered for Computer Vision).
  • Industry Talks: Interested alumni who work in industry or company representatives (i.e., ThetaData) can speak about the work they are doing, how they started working on that project, and how students can enter the field or work on similar projects.
  • Career Oriented Event: We've previously worked with David Belson from the career center and the COS Council to host panel discussions and presentations about the interview process. Some topics to discuss include what to do as a freshman or sophomore who hasn't secured an internship or how to prepare for the FAANG/Quant cycle next year.
  • Interest Groups: We currently support a few weekly subgroups, such as Princeton Competitive Programming (led by Prof. Paredes) and Plaintext (led by Raheem Idowu), which host weekly events for students interested in competitive programming or cryptography. This semester, we may also support an undergraduate NLP reading group (led by Brandon Cho) and potentially an undergraduate CV reading group (led by David Yan), if funds and interest permit.
  • Lab Visits: We'll host small sessions with grad students in their lab where undergraduates can see what a day (hour) in the life of a graduate student looks like!

ACM Trivia

Test your knowledge about the Association for Computing Machinery!

Which of these are ACM publications?
Communications of the ACM, ACM Transactions on Graphics, and Computing Reviews are all ACM publications. Journal of Computer Science is not an ACM publication.
When was ACM founded?
ACM was founded in 1947, making it one of the oldest scientific and educational computing societies in the world.
What prestigious award does ACM present annually for contributions to computing?
The ACM A.M. Turing Award, often referred to as the "Nobel Prize of Computing," is awarded annually by ACM to an individual for contributions of lasting and major technical importance to computing.
